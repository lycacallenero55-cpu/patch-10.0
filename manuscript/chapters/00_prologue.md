# PATCH 10.0
## Prologue — The Red Desert

The world had six hours left, and Sergeant Cha was worried about the well.

"Hold the brace," she said, and he held it, and she leaned her whole weight into the wrench until the bolt gave up arguing. Rust drifted off the scaffold like red snow. Forty meters below, in the dark of the shaft, water made a sound too small for anyone else to hear. He heard it. He heard most things.

"Two more days," she said, wiping her hands on her thighs. "Two more days and the twins get to drink something that didn't come off a tanker. You believe that?"

"I believe the well," he said.

"But not the two days."

He didn't answer. Over the west ridge, the sun was going down the way it had gone down all week — and then, for half a heartbeat, it *skipped*. A dropped frame in a reel only he was watching. The light stuttered from gold to deeper gold, and the wind forgot itself, and then everything resumed as if embarrassed.

Eighth time. He knew the tells the way farmers knew rain.

In that world his name was Han — just Han, a callsign, one syllable that fit in a shout. He was eighty-two years old in a body that looked forty and moved like it had been built by someone with strong opinions about waste. He had a pack on his shoulder. He'd had it on his shoulder for nine days. Nobody in the settlement asked about it anymore. The sergeant had asked exactly once, a year ago, and he had told her the truth, because he was tired in a way that had nothing to do with sleep.

*The world ends*, he'd said. *It has ended seven times. It gets rewritten — new place, new history, new everyone. Nobody remembers. I do.*

She'd looked at him for a long moment across the cookfire. Flint-eyed woman, jaw like a door that didn't open for strangers. Then she'd said: "That why you dig like that?"

"Like what?"

"Like the work's the last one."

He hadn't known what to say to that. She'd handed him the wrench. That had been the whole conversation, and it was more belief than eighty years had gotten him from anyone else.

Now she sat on the scaffold's edge with her boots hanging over forty meters of nothing, and she said, "Tonight, then."

"Tonight."

"You can tell? Down to the day?"

"Down to the hour, by the end. The wind quits early. The stars come up in the wrong order and fix themselves when you look." He nodded at the horizon. "The sun's been stuttering since Tuesday."

Sergeant Cha considered the sun, which had betrayed no opinion. She was quiet for a while. The desert did its evening thing, the long red exhale, heat leaving the land like a crowd leaving a stadium.

"Does it hurt?" she asked.

"No."

"Fast?"

"Fast."

"Then what's that face for?"

"It doesn't hurt," Han said. "That's the worst part. You'd think the end of a world would have the decency to."

She snorted. It was almost a laugh; she rationed those. Below them the water ticked and whispered against stone. Somewhere behind, in the settlement, someone was hammering tin, and the twins were chalking their names on the water tank again where the foreman kept scrubbing them off, and eleven households were cooking six dinners, because that was the arithmetic of the year, and all of it — every pot, every name, every debt and grudge and kindness — had six hours left, and only two people on the planet knew, and only one of them would remember.

"Don't tell the next me," she said.

He looked at her.

"When it happens." She was watching the ridge, not him. "Whatever I turn into — shopkeeper, schoolteacher, some soft woman in a soft place. Don't come find her and tell her about this." A pause. "If I hear ten good years and feel nothing, I'll have been made a liar. I'd rather be a stranger than a liar."

"You won't remember being either."

"*You* will," said Sergeant Cha. "So that's the order. Make it one you follow."

The sun went down without further incident. They finished the brace by lamplight because she insisted — *if the world ends tonight, I'd rather it catch me building* — and he held the last bolt steady and thought that in eighty years he had met perhaps four people worth outliving a world for, and that he had outlived three of them twice.

At 11:58 the wind quit.

At 11:59 the stars shuffled, guiltily, into a stranger's arrangement.

Han stood, and put both hands on the strap of his pack.

That was the discipline. Both hands on the strap. He had learned it the way he learned everything now — once, terribly. There had been a night, three worlds ago, when he had held on to something other than the strap, and what the wave had left beside him afterward he did not think about while sleeping, if he could help it. Living things could not be anchored. The world took them regardless, and if you held on, it took them *incorrectly*.

So: both hands on the strap. It was the hardest thing he did that decade.

"Sergeant," he said. "Thank you for the well."

She frowned at him, flint to the last. "The well's not done—"

The sky tore.

It did not roar. That was the obscenity of it — a seam of white static opened from horizon to horizon, silent as a held breath, like the spine of the world showing through its skin. The wave came across the desert at the speed of forgetting. It did not destroy anything. That would have been mercy, and mercy was not in the changelog. It *revised*. The dunes rolled over into boulevards. The watchtower shrugged and became a clock tower, and had always been one. The chalk names on the water tank became a mural of a city seal, sponsored by a bank that was older than the desert had ever been.

Sergeant Cha was still talking. That was the last thing. She was mid-sentence, wrench in hand, saying, "When the well's done, you should—"

The wave went through her like evening light through a window.

"—come by before we close, student," said a woman in a doorway, in an apron, under a paper lantern, on a street that smelled of broth and rain on warm stone. "First bowl's on the house for exam week."

She smiled at him — polite, proprietary, the smile you give a customer you haven't met — and went inside, and the door chimed, and the city of Haeseong went on humming around him, ten years old and two centuries deep, every brick certain it had always been there.

Han stood on the clean pavement with red dust on his boots. The only red dust in the world.

He knelt, after a while. Swept a pinch of it into his canteen with the edge of his palm, the way you'd gather up something that had spilled. Screwed the cap down carefully.

Then he shouldered the bag, and went looking for a bed.

*School*, the sky had said. He'd need a uniform.
