# Chapter 1 — The Ninth World

*Ten years later.*

Seo Han had failed forty-seven practical examinations in a row, and he was, if he said so himself, getting rather good at it.

The trick was never the losing. Anyone could lose. The trick was losing *believably* — at the correct speed, by the correct margin, with the correct amount of visible effort — so that no proctor in ten years had once leaned forward and thought, *wait.* Losing convincingly to talented children was, he had come to feel, the most technically demanding swordsmanship of his life, and he had studied under a man who could cut rain.

His opponent this morning made it harder than usual, because his opponent was Yoon Si-woo, and Yoon Si-woo was trying to help him.

"Senior." The boy's practice blade came up into second guard, humming faintly where his breath-phrase touched the steel. Mana ran along the fuller in a thin gold line, bright and eager, like everything else about him. "You're dropping your elbow again. If you keep the elbow in, your third beat won't collapse."

"I see," said Seo Han, whose third beat had been collapsing on schedule, by design, at the same angle, for two semesters.

"It's just — you're *almost* there. Everyone says theory students can't fight, but your bones are good. Master Im says bones don't lie."

"Mm. And yet mine have been failing practicals for three years."

"Because your elbow drops!"

He was a good boy. That was the whole trouble with him. Sixteen, sword-mad, kind the way weather is weather — Si-woo telegraphed kindness the way most people telegraphed attacks, a full beat early, from the shoulders. In the Etude halls they called him a middling talent. The assessors clocked his mana volume, his pitch control, his conservatory scores, and wrote *adequate*. None of their instruments measured what the boy actually had, which was that he meant every single thing he did. There was a version of swordsmanship where that mattered more than pitch. It had died two worlds ago. Seo Han did not say so.

The proctor called positions. Around the sparring floor, the morning's other bouts wound down; the hall smelled of resin and floor wax, and sunlight came down through high windows in long clean blades, and somewhere in the rafters the academy's sound-crystals hummed the standing chord that kept the practice wards up. A soft world. It had taken him two years to stop flinching at how soft.

"Rematch after class?" Si-woo whispered, as they saluted. "Just to fix the elbow. Ten minutes."

"One more," Seo Han said. "I'll be quick."

He was. Quickly into second guard, quickly pressured, quickly overwhelmed — the elbow dipping at the third beat like a tide answering a moon, the gold line of the boy's Etude singing past his guard and stopping, courteous, a centimeter off his collarbone.

"Point and match," said the proctor, and then, with the weariness of ritual: "Seo Han. Practical: fail. Attendance: perfect."

"Thank you," said Seo Han, politely, as he had forty-seven times.

He collected the loss the way he collected all of them, with a small bow to his opponent — and it was in the bow, head lowered, that Si-woo said the thing.

"Senior, can I ask you something weird?" The boy was rewrapping his grip, not looking at him, embarrassed in advance. "Do you ever have a dream that feels — used? Like it belonged to somebody first?"

"Describe it."

"Red sand," Si-woo said. "All the way out. The sky feels — too big, like it'd hate a roof. And I'm digging. There's a crew of us and we're digging for water, and I want to finish so badly I could cry, which is stupid, it's a *dream*. And there's a water tank." He laughed at himself, apologetic. "This is the weird part. There are two names chalked on the tank. Kids' handwriting. Somebody keeps scrubbing them off and they keep coming back, and in the dream I think — *good.* Every night for a week now. I've never even seen a desert."

The hall went on being sunny. The crystals went on humming their soft chord. Seo Han stood very still inside his own body, which is a thing a man can learn to do, given ninety years and sufficient cause, and he took inventory of his voice before he spent any of it.

"What color was the chalk?"

"White," Si-woo said. "No — one name white, one yellow. Why?"

"No reason. Dreams borrow," said Seo Han. "Yours has good taste in skies."

He picked up his failing grade and his practice sword and walked out into the ninth world, where the bell was ringing the half hour, and everything was fine.

---

Everything was not fine. He had known for nine days.

Haeseong in festival season was a city practicing its own smile. Banners went up along the tram lines — SORYEONG ACADEMY: TWO HUNDRED YEARS OF THE GRAND CHORUS — and paper lanterns accumulated in the plane trees like migrating birds, and first-years chalked recital adverts on the pavement outside the conservatory gates. The academy was two hundred years old the way he was twenty: convincingly. He had once spent a pleasant winter afternoon in the archives reading two centuries of founding documents, admiring the work. The handwriting aged. The ink faded on schedule. The world was a better forger than he would ever be a liar, and it never got cocky, and it never confessed. He'd gone back to his dormitory that evening with something almost like professional respect.

He walked the long way through the market, because it was Tuesday, and on Tuesdays he did his rounds.

The tells were all where he'd left them. At 3:07 exactly, the birds stopped — every sparrow in the plane trees going silent between one wingbeat and the next, four seconds, five, and then resuming their argument as if nothing had happened. Nobody looked up. Nobody ever looked up. At the corner of the fish street, the rice-cake vendor greeted him with, "Cold for the season, isn't it? My knees knew before the radio did," which was word for word, pause for pause, what she had said to him on Saturday — the world sampling itself, the way a tired musician repeats a phrase and trusts no one's listening closely. He bought two rice cakes. He always bought two when she glitched. It seemed rude not to.

Nine days of this, by his ledger. The wind quitting early in the evenings. The moon coming up with a stutter, a half-second skip, like a film catching on the reel — he'd timed it off the harbor clocktower three nights running. Every world announced its end to exactly one resident, in the same patient grammar, and after eight endings he could read the countdown the way the sergeant had read weather.

Festival night, said the ledger. Nine days. He had planned accordingly: what to carry, which door to leave by, where a man should stand when a world went under so that he'd surface in the next one on his feet, alone, unremarked. There was an arithmetic to the end of everything and he was, God help him, fluent in it.

He ate one rice cake and wrapped the other, and went to dinner early, because it was Tuesday.

---

Cha's Kitchen sat in the alley behind the tram depot, six stools and a counter and a paper lantern the color of a low fire, and it had been there thirty years, and he had watched it be there for ten of them.

"The usual, student?"

"Please."

The woman who ran it had forearms like dock rope and a way of moving through her tiny kitchen that suggested a much larger space had once been under her command, though if you asked her — he never would again — she'd tell you she had run this shop since before he was born, inherited from an aunt, and the ledger of her life closed flush on every page. She drove the cleaver through spring onions the way some people drove bolts, from the shoulder, economical. There was an old callus on her left hand, at the base of the fingers, exactly where a torque wrench wears one. No noodle in the world puts that callus on a woman's hand.

The patch had rewritten her decade, her trade, her name, her aunt into existence. It had not managed to rewrite the way she salted broth. He had eaten in nine worlds. He would have known that broth blindfolded, in any of them, under any sky the changelog cared to hang.

"You're thin," she informed him, setting down the bowl. "Exam week?"

"Festival week."

"Same thing. Eat."

He ate. She went back to her cutting board, and the radio mumbled about festival road closures, and steam climbed the walls, and for eleven minutes the ninth world was, on balance, a good one.

He left the exact money, and then, under the bowl where she wouldn't find it until he was gone, the tip she always refused.

*For the well,* he didn't say.

"Study hard, student," she said, not looking up. "Come by before closing on festival night. First bowl's on the house."

"I'll be quick," said Seo Han, and stepped out under the lantern, and stood a moment in the alley, and let the door chime settle, and filed the evening where he filed all of them — in the black ledger of things worth outliving a world for, credits column, page ninety-one.

---

His dormitory room was on the top floor of the oldest hall, chosen for its sight lines, its two exits, and its unpopularity. He had lived in it for ten years, and it contained, by design, almost nothing: a bed, a desk, a tea set, a locked case.

The tea came first. It always came first. Three breaths to warm the pot, pour from the height of a hand's width, no higher — *tea poured from arrogance tastes of it* — the little liturgy of a man who had been dead for seventy years and a world that had never existed. His master had beaten exactly two things into him that no patch had ever managed to collect: a sword, and a way of making tea. Seo Han performed the second nightly. The first he performed only in his sleep now, forms in the dark of him, kata like buried rivers.

Then he unlocked the case.

Eight bundles of oilcloth, each tied with the same knot. He didn't open them every night. Tonight was for checking the wrapping — nine days out, by the ledger — but a man checking wrapping is a man remembering, whatever he tells himself.

A rusted sword guard, iron, chrysanthemum-cut. *The mountain that taught me. The man who could cut rain.*

A ring of chalk-white stone that had once been a promise between apprentices. *The tower. The eight-year siege. Ille, who laughed at her own funerals.*

A censer coin stamped with a god's closed eye. *The debt that outlived its creditor.*

A pair of dog tags, one edge melted smooth.

He did not narrate the tags. Even inwardly, even now. There was a door in him where Go Beom-seok's name was posted, and behind that door, a night three worlds ago: the wave coming across the gate-district at the speed of forgetting, and his own hand closing on his captain's arm — *hold on, brother, hold on to me* — because he had been eighty years old and still stupid enough to think the rule had exceptions. Living things cannot be anchored. The wave does not make exceptions. It makes *compromises*. What stood up beside him afterward had worn Beom-seok's face over something the new world had half-finished writing, and it had looked at him with two men's eyes, and it had asked him — politely, in a voice tuned between two voices — which name it should answer to.

He had answered the only way that was left to answer.

Both hands on the strap. That was the whole of the law now. He checked the knot on the tags twice and set them down gently, the way you set down a sleeping thing, and moved on, because moving on was also a discipline.

A matte-black chip the size of a thumbnail, port-scarred. *Eighty-four years of service, seventeen wars, one goodbye pending.*

A key that opened nothing. He checked its knot without unwrapping it all the way. Some doors you check are checked from the *outside*.

A dented canteen, silent when shaken, one-third full of red dust. *The well's not done.*

And last, smallest, a silver hairpin.

It was a plain thing. No maker's mark, no ornament except a single flower at the head, so worn by handling that the species was gone — five petals or six, he'd argued both ways across whole decades. He had no story for it. That was the strangeness he'd stopped looking at directly, the way you stop seeing a crack in a familiar wall: eight bundles, and he could give a eulogy for seven. The hairpin he had simply always had. Older than the sword guard. Older than his oldest inventory, which was itself older than any world currently on record. He had carried it the longest and knew it the least, and once — one bad winter, three or four worlds back — he had tried to leave it behind on principle, walked six streets, and turned around with his heart going like a struck bell, and never tried again.

He wrapped it. Tied the knot.

*Ninety years,* he thought, without drama, the way a quartermaster thinks. *One bag.*

---

The library annex stayed open late in festival season, and he had a corner in the acoustics stacks where nobody ever came.

Somebody had come.

"Senior. Settle an argument for me."

Yoo Ha-eun, third-year, transferred in that spring from a coastal school nobody had heard of and nobody ever pressed about, because her marks were the kind that ended conversations about credentials. She had a stack of restricted tower-survey folios open across his table — his table — and a tuning fork, and the self-possession of a person who has already decided how this exchange will go and is merely waiting for you to catch up.

"The old bell towers," she said. "Harbor, garrison, and the one in the academy's founding quad. I've measured all three this month. They hum flat. Not badly cast — *consistently* flat, all three, a quarter-tone under the civic standard, as if they were tuned true against some other standard and the city moved underneath them." She looked at him, and it was a level look, gray-eyed, unhurried. "Professor Im says mana has always sung and always will, one Chorus, one pitch. Fine. Then why do the oldest things in this city sing a quarter-tone off, senior? What were they tuned *to*?"

There were perhaps four people alive who could have asked a more dangerous question, and none of them knew they were alive.

"Old bronze drifts," said Seo Han.

"Uniformly? All three? In tune with *each other* and flat to everything else?" She tapped the folio. "That's not drift. Drift disagrees with itself. This agrees. Things that agree were tuned together." She let that sit exactly one beat too long. "In a room I can't find."

"You've been climbing bell towers at night."

"I've been *surveying* bell towers at night. There's paperwork." A pause. "Some of it's even filed."

He was, against every regulation he had written for himself, close to smiling. He liked her the way you liked a well-made blade in someone else's scabbard — with appreciation, from a distance, alert to the fact of the edge. Ten years in the ninth world and she was the first person to walk up to one of its seams and put a finger on it and *push*.

"Ask Professor Im," he said, standing, gathering his notes.

"I did. She gave me the Chorus catechism and a biscuit." Ha-eun turned a page of the folio without looking at it. Then, absently — and this was the thing, it was *absent*, it cost her nothing, she didn't even hear herself doing it — she began to hum.

Four notes. Down, down, a lift, a fall. A little worn tune, the kind that lives in kitchens, in work, in the space between someone's attention and their hands.

In four notes, ninety years of indexed memory came apart and reassembled itself, and found nothing.

He knew the tune. He knew it the way he knew broth, the way he knew his master's tea — bone-deep, provenance-certain — and it had no provenance. It was not the wasteland; he could sing the wasteland's whole songbook. Not the gate-districts, not the tower, not the mountain, not the drowned hymns of the god-years, not anything from the black decade he did not hum from on purpose. Nine worlds of music, shelved and dated in a memory that did not lose things, and the tune fit none of them. It fit somewhere *older*.

There was nowhere older.

The scale was wrong, too — wrong for this world, a tuning the Chorus had never bothered to invent, and it sat against the ear the way the bell towers sat against the city: true to a standard that wasn't there.

Her pen stopped. She'd noticed him.

"Sorry," she said. "Habit. It gets stuck in my head."

"What is it?"

"No idea. Dreamed it, I think." She frowned at the tuning fork, and for one moment the self-possession flickered — not to shyness; to something more careful, like a person feeling for a step in the dark. "Senior. This will sound strange."

"I doubt that."

"Haven't we met? Before spring. Before this school." She said it plainly, as a datum, the way she'd reported the flat towers. "I don't mean it as a line. I mean I keep — expecting you to already know things I haven't told you."

"I have one of those faces," said Seo Han.

Ha-eun looked at him for a moment with the full survey-grade attention she had been spending on the folios, and he endured it the way he endured proctors and customs officers and one memorable exorcist, ninety years of practice at being unremarkable arranged politely behind his eyes.

"No," she said at last, gently, as if correcting a measurement. "You don't."

She went back to her folios. He went out into the corridor, and stood in the lamplight a moment, and realized his thumb was resting against the outline of the case key in his pocket — the way it did, he noted with clinical interest, perhaps twice a decade.

---

On the roof of the old hall, at 11:58, he checked the moon.

This was the routine of the last nine days: harbor clocktower in the left eye, moonrise over the water in the right, the jeweler's watch open in his palm. One skip a night, half a second, regular as rent. Six more skips to festival night. The arithmetic of the end of everything, and he fluent in it, and the bag packed, and the doors chosen, and every farewell already being paid out in installments small enough that no one would ever total them.

The moon came up off the water, caught—

—skipped—

—and skipped *again*.

Twice. A full second gone, the reflection snapping across the harbor like a dropped stitch. Below, in the plane trees, every bird went silent at midnight, which was seven hours off schedule, and did not resume.

Seo Han stood on the roof of the ninth world with the watch ticking in his hand and did the sums twice, because he had not survived eight endings by trusting a first draft. The count was not the count. The tells were compounding. Nine days had become — he watched the moon steady itself, watched the harbor light stop shivering — three. Perhaps two, if the acceleration held.

For the first time in ninety years, the world was running early.

He closed the watch. Stood a while in the wind that had quit too soon, above the city practicing its smile, festival banners breathing along the tram lines — two hundred years of history, ten years old, seventy-two hours to live. Down in the alley behind the tram depot a paper lantern was still lit, the color of a low fire. First bowl's on the house, she'd said. Festival night.

She was going to miss their last dinner by two days, and there was no one in any world he could tell.

"Early," he said to the ninth world, quietly, the way you'd note a guest arriving before the table was set. "You couldn't give me the nine days."

The world, a better forger than he would ever be a liar, said nothing, and kept its smile on, and somewhere below a banner-rope creaked like a scaffold.

He went inside to pack.
